Entry 2: AI block reflection

Context

For this block, our group designed a language teaching activity that combined AI tools, Copilot and Suno, to promote creativity, vocabulary learning, and collaboration. The activity follows a structured sequence that integrates language production with technology in a meaningful way. First, students work in pairs to do a worksheet and to play a game related to Halloween words. Then, they are assigned three Halloween-related words (a noun, a verb, and an adjective) and they have to write a short verse of a song using them and Copilot. After that, they join another pair with the same words to combine their verses and create a longer one that becomes part of the class Halloween song. Afterwards, the class uses Copilot to turn this verses into song lyrics and Suno to create the song with the voice and the instruments. Later, each group produces a version of their paragraph with missing words to be used in a listening activity using the generated song, allowing them to assess their understanding of the content. Following that, students review the song and practise performing it. Then, they design scaffolding materials to present and sing the song to the kindergarten children of the school. Finally, the project concludes with an individual self-assessment, where each student reflects on their learning, participation, and use of technology throughout the process.

Critical reflection

This project was an enriching experience because it showed how AI can be integrated into language teaching in creative yet structured ways. One of the main challenges was designing an activity that balanced linguistic objectives with the use of AI tools. We needed to make sure students were not only playing with technology, but actually using it to learn and communicate meaningfully in English. On the one hand, several aspects of the plan worked particularly well. First, the progressive structure of the activity allowed for a smooth integration of language learning and AI use. Starting with a vocabulary-based worksheet and game created a meaningful context for introducing Halloween-related words, while the prompt creation with Copilot encouraged guided creativity and vocabulary practise. Then, the collaborative step, where pairs joined others with the same words to merge their verses, added a cooperative dimension that promoted peer learning and co-construction of meaning. Furthermore, the decision to use Copilot for generating verses and Suno for composing the final song gave the project a clear and motivating technological purpose. Moreover, these tools not only supported creativity but also demonstrated how AI can be meaningfully used to produce authentic and engaging language content. In addition, the inclusion of a listening comprehension activity and the final stage of designing materials for kindergarten learners showed how the activity connected production, comprehension, and application in a coherent way. On the other hand, we also identified some limitations during the planning process. One issue was ensuring that all students would contribute equally to the prompt writing stage, as some learners prefer to let others do all the work, and others do not let students participate. Another potential difficulty was managing the technological side, making sure every student had access to their account, and the internet connection remained stable. Additionally, we realized that without guidance, students might produce only minimal or disconnected verses. According to the feedback from our teacher, we introduced several key changes that made the activity clearer and more effective. First, we decided that the teacher would create individual accounts for each pair on Copilot and Suno before the class, ensuring smoother access and saving time.

We also adjusted the structure so that students first worked in pairs and later joined another pair with the same words, promoting richer collaboration and allowing them to co-create longer verses together.

Another major change was improving the prompt guide. To make sure students didn’t write only one sentence, we added the step to make them write an example sentence using the assigned words (a noun, verb, and adjective) before generating their verse with Copilot.

Moreover, we also modified the fact that each pair had to create a different song and that the whole class had to select the best one, because it was too complex. Now, each pair had to create one verse, and, with another pair, a final one. Finally, they would have to combine all the verses into a single song.

Furthermore, we also implemented a new task where students created a fill-in-the-gaps worksheet for their verse and then used it in a listening activity with the generated song. This not only helped them assess their comprehension but also positioned them as teachers of their own content.

Based on the feedback from our classmates, we refined the activity even further. We added a teacher’s demonstration song before starting, so students could see a complete example of the process. Moreover, we included clearer instructions on how to use Copilot, planned vocabulary support for unknown words, and defined specific roles for each group member during the stage of creating the prompt to ensure equal participation. Finally, we designed a Plan B in case of internet connection problems.

Link to TPACK Competences

This project helped me reinforce several areas of my TPACK competences. Before this activity, I felt more confident with basic tools like Google Docs or Canva, but now I can see how AI can be integrated meaningfully into language learning tasks. I learned how to design an activity where technology supports not just motivation, but also linguistic and communicative goals. Moreover, working with AI made me reflect on the ethical and pedagogical responsibilities of teachers when introducing these tools in class. I gained a deeper understanding of both the potential and limitations of AI in education, because while it can inspire creativity and engagement, it sometimes generates inaccurate or biased outputs. This reminded me that as a future teacher, I must guide students to use AI critically, questioning, contrasting, and verifying information rather than accepting it uncritically. So, it is not only about knowing how to use technology, but also about guiding students to understand its limits, question its outputs, and use it as a support for their own learning rather than a shortcut. Through this experience, I developed a better understanding of how to adapt technology to different learning contexts, anticipate technical challenges, and scaffold tasks so that technology truly enhances learning. This aligns with my goal of becoming more confident and critical in using advanced digital tools in my future teaching practice.

My reflective song

To conclude my reflection, I decided to use what I explained and create a song using Copilot to generate the lyrics and Suno to add the voice and instrumentation. I chose this format because it represents the core idea of our project, that is integrating technology, creativity, and learning in meaningful ways.

Entry 3: 360 block reflection

To present my reflection in a visual, structured, and thematic way, I created a ThingLink that organizes the different parts of my analysis. However, since my account will be permanently deleted in 30 days and the ThingLink will disappear with it, what I present here is a video in which I walk through the interactive map and show how it is organized. The ThingLink maintains the space-themed atmosphere of the original activity, turning the reflection into a journey through space where each stop reveals a different aspect of my learning process.

The following written reflection expands on the ideas presented in the interactive map, offering a deeper and more detailed analysis of the project. It examines the evolution of the initial concept, the elements that contributed to the activity’s success, the limitations identified during implementation, and the improvements that would enhance future versions. It also considers the development of my TPACK competences throughout this process.

Context

For this project, we designed a technology and language learning activity using Thinglink 360° images. The activity, titled “Searching for a new planet,” was created for sixth-grade learners (A2 CEFR level) and aimed to integrate language skills (listening, speaking, reading, and writing) within a highly visual and interactive environment. The main goal of the task was to engage students in a space exploration mission, where they acted as astronauts searching for a new planet for humans to live on. Each group had to explore a 360° image of a different fictional planet, gathering information about its living conditions. Then, they had to create both a written report and an audio recording summarizing their findings, which they later had to give with another group, so they could have more information. Finally, in a class discussion, students compared the different planets and collectively decided which one was most suitable for human life. The design followed a pre-task, main task, and post-task phases. During the pre-task, students were introduced to the problem (Earth becoming uninhabitable) through a short news text and created a visual glossary of key vocabulary. In the main task, they explored their assigned planet through the Thinglink 360° image, which contained clues and information. Moreover, scaffolding materials (e.g., report models, checklists, and sentence starters) guided them through the process. In the post-task phase, the whole class shared and discussed their findings, using comparison structures to reach a final decision. This project was designed not only to develop students’ linguistic competences, particularly descriptive and comparative language, but also to foster critical thinking, collaboration, and digital literacy. It provided students with a meaningful communicative purpose while allowing them to experience immersive technology in an educational context.

Critical reflection

When we first started planning the project, our initial idea was quite different. We had originally thought about designing an activity in which students would explore the 360° planets and describe the aliens living on them. The focus was going to be on using adjectives and descriptive language to talk about the aliens’ appearance, behaviour, and habitats, and then exchange those descriptions orally with their peers. However, as we discussed this idea in the group with our teacher, we realized that although it was imaginative, it lacked a strong communicative purpose and real-life relevance. Describing aliens might be fun, but it does not connect easily with situations that students might encounter in their daily lives or in realistic communicative contexts. Moreover, it did not provide a clear narrative or task outcome that would keep students motivated throughout the whole activity. After reflecting on this, we decided to do the activity around something that would still be creative but more authentic and meaningful. That is why we thought of the idea of conducting a mission to find a new planet for humans to live on. This shift gave the project a clearer goal and allowed us to include a report-writing element, which not only developed descriptive language but also introduced students to a real-world text type. Instead of describing imaginary creatures, students now had to describe elements such as food, weather, water, size, and living conditions, using language that can be transferred to real-life communication, for example when describing a city, a country, or even a person’s lifestyle.

I think this change was one of the most successful decisions in our design process. The final activity had a strong narrative thread, clear linguistic goals, and a combination of skills that reflected authentic communication. The students’ task had purpose, coherence, and a sense of accomplishment.

What worked

After putting the activity into practice with our classmates, we were able to test the first part of the main task, where participants explored the planets on Thinglink and began completing the report worksheet. Even though we could not finish the whole task cycle, this classroom practice gave us valuable insights into how the activity worked in practice. One of the strongest aspects was the topic itself, which turned out to be very engaging. The idea of searching for a new planet combines imagination, adventure, and scientific curiosity, three elements that naturally capture students’ attention. Moreover, the use of Thinglink 360° significantly enhanced that engagement. The immersive environment allowed participants to explore each planet as if they were really there, clicking on hotspots to discover information and visual details. This level of interactivity created a sense of autonomy and exploration, transforming the lesson into an experience rather than a simple classroom task.

Another major strength was the scaffolding. The report model, the checklist, the description scaffolding, the discussion scaffolding and the comparison and superlatives scaffolding helped participants understand the task and produce well-structured output. Our classmates commented that the scaffolding made the task accessible and well-organized, allowing them to focus on language use rather than worrying about what to do next.

What did not work

Since we only completed the first part of the main task, we could not test the audio exchange or the final discussion. However, from what we observed and from the feedback received, I identified two aspects that could be improved. First, the oral scaffolding could be developed further, as we have not added it. While the written scaffolds worked very well, I think students might still struggle during the audio-recording phase, especially with pronunciation, and fluency. Without explicit oral support, some students may find it difficult to perform the speaking task confidently. Second, even though when we presented the activity to our classmates everything was clear and well understood, I thought that for younger learners (sixth graders) it might be a bit more confusing to follow all the steps of the main task. For that reason, I believe it would be useful to create short written or visual instructions indicating what to do at each stage (e.g., first explore, then complete the checklist, then write, then record). Perhaps the teacher’s explanations would already make everything clear, but having those step-by-step guidelines would ensure that students do not get lost during the task.

How I would improve it (and why)

Based on what I observed during the classroom practice and on our reflections afterward, I believe several improvements could make the project more accessible, smooth, and complete. First, adding oral scaffolding would support students during the audio phase; including short sample recordings or AI-generated pronunciation models could help them prepare with greater confidence and fluency. I would also provide step-by-step task instructions through a short guide outlining what students need to do at each stage of the main task. This would help younger learners stay focused and avoid confusion, even when the teacher is not intervening directly. Another important adjustment would be preparing a “Plan B” for moments of limited connectivity by designing printable versions of the planets, either through screenshots or brief text descriptions, so the activity can still run smoothly in case of internet or technical issues. Finally, although we already apply hetero-assessment and self-assessment, I believe adding a peer-assessment component during the audio phase would be beneficial. This would allow students to feel they are supporting one another while learning even more, and it would encourage them to reflect on pronunciation, clarity, and use of the target language, all while practicing active listening.

Link to my TPACK competences

This project has been an important step forward in the development of my TPACK competences. In my first reflections, I recognized that I was still building confidence with AR tools. Through this experience, I was finally able to apply such technology meaningfully, not just experiment with it. My Technological Knowledge (TK) expanded as I learned to design and navigate Thinglink 360°, customize interactive images, and anticipate potential classroom management challenges. My Pedagogical Knowledge (PK) was reinforced by aligning the use of technology with CEFR descriptors and task-based learning principles, ensuring that every digital element had a clear educational purpose. At the same time, my Content Knowledge (CK) was deepened as I reflected on how to balance creativity with language usefulness, from example, shifting from “describing aliens” to writing functional reports. Compared with my previous projects, this experience shows a clear evolution from being cautious with advanced technology to using it confidently and critically. I am now more aware that effective technology integration is not about making activities look innovative, but about using digital tools to create authentic, engaging, and linguistically purposeful experiences. I feel that I am developing into a more reflective teacher who can adapt technology to different contexts, design meaningful tasks, and scaffold students’ learning in multimodal environments. This project has strengthened my confidence and confirmed that immersive technologies, when used thoughtfully, can become powerful allies for language learning.
